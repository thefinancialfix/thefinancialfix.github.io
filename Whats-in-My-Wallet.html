What's in My Wallet?

An overview of my everyday lineup of plastic.

Posted by Dan Fooks on October 9, 2019

Although I’m definitely a saver and NOT a spender, everyone needs to spend money – some for necessities, and some for fun. Groceries, gas, and textbooks are just some expenses that a college student like myself might incur. However, when spending money, I am careful and sure to save as much money as possible by utilizing multiple credit cards. This article’s sole purpose is to provide an overview of what I carry in my wallet, and an explanation of how certain cards provide me with certain benefits.


Cash

Let’s start with the obvious – cash. I usually like to carry anywhere from $60-$100 in cash on a day-to-day basis. Although I almost always use a credit card, you never know when you’ll need cash, so it’s always good to have some on hand. I can’t tell you how many times I’ve been out with friends or out to eat and needed cash to make change or give a tip. I’m always sure to carry a few 1s and 5s so I can make change for just about any amount. It’s true that cash is king, so don’t forget to always have some on hand.


TD Bank Contactless Debit Card

I carry my debit card for one sole purpose, which is to deposit and withdrawal from a TD ATM. I’ll never make purchases with my debit card, as credit cards are not only more secure, but also earn points on spending. If I have the opportunity to pay with a credit card, I always will. My debit card pretty much collects dust in my wallet due to the fact that I rarely need to use the ATM. Just like cash, it is always good to have in my wallet in case I need quick access to cash. My specific debit card, the TD Bank Contactless Debit Card, is no different from any bank’s debit card (it does have tap-to-pay technology, which is cool, but again, I never use it). I will say, if you bank with TD Bank, it looks a lot nicer than the standard debit card that is distributed when you create an account with TD. Read more about this card here.

Chase Freedom Card

Although I initially applied for the Chase freedom solely for the signup bonus ($150 cash back after spending $500), this card has proved to be very useful in my lineup. Starting in 2018, Chase started offering 5% back on gas twice per year for two quarters (this was the first time they offered gas for two quarters since 2012). The first and third quarter of the year are typically gas stations and something else, such as drug stores or streaming services. The second quarter has typically been grocery stores, which works great for me. As a college student, I spend on a weekly basis at the grocery store, so it helps to be earning a little cash back for spending that is already occurring, anyway. I typically spend anywhere from $100-$150 in groceries per month, so earning 5% puts a nice amount of money back in my pocket. The fourth quarter for this card is usually something like wholesale clubs or Walmart. The nicest thing about this card is that its categories do not overlap with those of the Discover it card. For example, While the Freedom earns 5% on gas for Q1 and Q3, Discover earns 5% on gas during Q2, which means you’re able to earn 5% cash back on gas for 9 months out of the year. Chase earns 5% on dining during Q2, while Discover earns 5% on dining during Q3, so you’re earning 5% on dining for 6 months out of the year. Moral of the story? This card works great in any wallet paired with the Discover it Card. You can sign up for the Chase Freedom card here.

Amazon Prime Rewards Visa Signature Card/Amazon Rewards Visa Signature Card

Although I have yet to use this credit card physically in person, the Amazon Prime Rewards Visa Signature Card (or the Amazon Rewards Visa Signature Card for those who don’t have Amazon Prime) is a must-have if you shop on Amazon. The Amazon Prime Visa earns a whopping 5% back on all Amazon.com purchases. Every time I order from Amazon, you can bet I’m using this credit card. In addition to a great savings on Amazon.com, this card also earns 2% back at gas stations, restaurants, and drugstores, and 1% back on everything else. When the Chase Freedom or Discover it isn’t earning me 5% back on gas in a given quarter, this card is my go-to for gas. Eventually, I intend to have a card that covers 5% back on gas for the entirety of the year, but cards with this specific perk are much harder to obtain. The card also earns 2% back on dining, which could come in handy if you don’t have a designated card for dining. Although this card technically has no annual fee, you must have a Prime account, which comes in at an annual cost of $119 ($59 for students). NOTE: If your parent has an Amazon Prime account, you are eligible to enjoy the benefits of Prime at no cost with Amazon Household, a platform that lets you share a Prime account between family members. Luckily, because my family uses Prime, I get to enjoy this credit card (and Amazon Prime) at no annual cost. You can check out the Amazon Prime Rewards Visa Signature Card here.

If you love Amazon as much as I do but don’t have a Prime membership, do not worry – there’s still a card for you. The Amazon Rewards Visa Signature is essentially the same card, minus the Prime aspect. With this card, you can benefit from the same great perks as the Prime version of this card, only the 5% you earn on Amazon with the Prime Visa card is knocked down to 3% with the standard version of this card. However, I still believe this is a great option, and an essential for those who shop on Amazon. You can check out the Amazon Rewards Visa Signature Card here.


Uber Visa Card

The Uber Visa credit card is definitely a little more uncommon (and slept on), as I have never ran into anyone else with this card. Although it is not my everyday card, the Uber Visa is by far my favorite card in my lineup. Let’s start by answering the first question that might pop into your head – No, I am not an Uber driver, and no, you don’t need to be an Uber driver to own this card. This card earns 4% cash back on dining and 2% back on online purchases (these are the only perks that pertain to myself). The card also earns 3% back on hotel and airfare, and 1% on everything else. Oddly enough, the Uber Visa card does not offer any special cashback for using Uber, but Uber is included as an online purchase, so you’re still earning 2% cash back while using the Uber app with this card. It’s great to have a card that earns 4% back on dining 12 months out of the year. Every time I’m out to eat, I know that I’m earning some solid cash back. If you’re going out to eat with friends, put the bill on your Uber Visa card and have them pay you in cash or on Venmo (I promise, the cash back adds up QUICK). I also use this card for any online purchases outside of Amazon.com. In addition to having great perks, this card has no annual fee, and currently offers a sign-up bonus of $100 cash back after spending $500 (20% cash back on the first $500 spent). You can check out more information on this card here.


Discover it Card

The Discover it card is a very solid card in my lineup, and I find myself using it for a good portion of the year. Upon getting approved for this card, Discover DOUBLES your cash back at the end of your first year. In other words, when you’re earning 5% back on rotational categories, you’re really earning 10% cash back, which is incredible. I used my cashback at the end of the first year to purchase a TV on Amazon, who gave me an additional 15% discount on Prime Day for using Discover! Additionally, This TV really had no out of pocket cost due to the fact that it was purchased solely using cashback. Similar to the Chase Freedom, Discover offers a rotation of quarterly categories that you can view here. You also earn 2% for the first year on all other purchases (because of the doubled cashback), which returns to the normal 1% after the first year. The Discover it card was my first credit card and is a great way to start building credit while earning points simultaneously, all while paying no annual fee. I’d recommend the Discover it as a great starter credit card for anyone looking to start building a credit score.
