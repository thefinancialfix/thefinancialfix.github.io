Robinhood Launches Cash Management with Debit Card and 1.80% APY

Learn more about Robinhood’s new Cash Management feature. With Robinhood offering no commission fees and 1.80% APY, brokerages are firing back.

Posted by Dan Fooks on November 6, 2019

A few months back, Robinhood announced they were launching a checking and savings platform, or cash management platform, which quickly fell through after Robinhood failed to obtain FDIC insurance. This would prove to be a problem in the long run, as to make this work, they would have to treat their checking and savings accounts as investment accounts to use SIPC insurance, a securities insurance, which wasn’t the best idea. Common banks are FDIC insured, which means that to release a banking platform, Robinhood should ideally be using FDIC insurance as well, as it has more extensive dollar for dollar coverage (Insured for up to $250,000 per depositor). SIPC insurance would prove to be a problem for getting money back quickly in the event of a financial crisis for Robinhood. Long story short, the insurance did not work out, and Robinhood cancelled cash management along with its 3% APY for the time being. Months later, Robinhood is back with a new offer on the table.

Robinhood has newly announced its revamped cash management platform, this time firing back with 2.05% APY and FDIC insurance. Robinhood has attempted to create a lot of buzz by bringing back the platform, and they have done exactly that. However, they have recently announced that they would be decreasing the APY rate from 2.05% to 1.80%, apparently due to the Federal Reserve decreasing the federal funds rate. Although the fed does directly affect APYs for banks, I personally believe that this was all part of Robinhood’s marketing plan, and they intended to lower the rate after drawing in a substantial amount of users. They probably would have lowered the rate from 3% if cash management had worked out at the beginning of 2019. Introduce higher than usual APY, draw in a bunch of millennials, and then lower APY. Well played, Robinhood, but I can see what’s going on. So what does a lower APY mean? Nothing much, just a little less money per year, which isn’t the end of the world. Let’s take a look at Robinhood’s past APY offers compared to its current APY offer.


Per Every $1000 Deposited:

3.0% APY - $30 during first year
2.05% APY - $20.50 during first year
1.80% APY - $18.00 during first year (current APY)


Compared to a standard bank’s APY, all of the above numbers are a significant upgrade. However, it is a little disappointing to see a decreasing APY over the course of a year. It would be nice to see Robinhood stick with a flat 2.0% APY, of course if they could afford to do so as a company.


Let’s go over some key features of Robinhood’s new Cash Management Platform:

Insurance – Robinhood Cash Management is equipped with FDIC insurance for up to $1.25 million in funds. This is extremely important, as your money is just as safe as it would be in a standard bank.

A More-Than-Competitive Interest Rate – While most banks offer an interest rate well under 1% (TD offers .05%), Robinhood Cash Management offers a 1.80% APY, which blows away a majority of competitors.

Debit Card – The Cash Management portion of Robinhood offers a debit card with a selection of different designs, that can be used anywhere Mastercard is accepted.

ATM Access – Access to over 75,000 in-network ATMs to withdrawal/deposit funds. These surcharge-free ATMs can be found in Wawa, Target, 7Eleven, and other convenience stores.



Other Brokerages Fire Back to Stay Afloat

With Robinhood previously being the only commonly known commission-free brokerage in existence, other brokerages view this as a concern, especially with Robinhood attracting the newer generation of traders and investors, while more traditional brokerages tend to target an older demographic. However, brokerages are beginning to compete in order to capture younger traders, and here’s how:

Starting on October 7th of this year, Charles Schwab announced they would be launching commission-free trading for its users. Immediately following this announcement, TD Ameritrade lost nearly 20% of its value. Naturally, the only thing TD Ameritrade could do is join Schwab in eliminating commission fees. Fidelity followed Schwab as well, and the competition began. Within the near future, we will most likely see a competition between brokerages to see who can offer the most to both new and existing traders. Maybe we will see features such as referral links or fractional trading (the ability to purchase a stock at a fraction of its price per share) in order to increase appeal to the new generation of investors. Traditional brokerages will possibly attempt to appeal to younger users, or maybe Robinhood will even make moves in order to bring more advanced features to its platform, such as technical analysis features. Cash management with an included debit card is a great example of enhanced user functionality that Robinhood is bringing to the table.



Will I Use Cash Management?

The short answer is yes (but no, but yes). Although I’ll be using Robinhood Cash Management, I will not be using Robinhood Cash Management as a “full-time bank”. I am a big advocate of banking with a company that has physical locations. This means I have access to in-person customer service, and instant deposits/withdrawals. I have banked with TD Bank for all my life, and have never had any issues, especially considering there are no fees or minimum balance requirements for students. Mobile banking platforms are seeming to get increasingly popular among the younger demographic. Platforms such as Chime intend to bring customers into the world of mobile banking, eliminating the idea of a physical banking location. However, I don’t want to lose the convenience of instant transfers, deposits, and in-person customer service, so I’ll be sticking with TD (which has a great mobile app). I will be using Robinhood Cash Management for one key feature: 1.80% APY. I won’t be transferring money in and out, and my direct deposits won’t be directed to Robinhood. I won’t be touching the debit card that comes with the account. However, I WILL be taking advantage of Robinhood’s higher-than-usual interest rate to store my emergency cash fund, money that would be sitting in my low-interest savings account otherwise, collecting dust. Although, I am disappointed because this 1.80% APY was originally supposed to be 3% APY months ago, which was bumped down to 2.05% more recently, and now is just 1.80% APY. The downside is that APY is subject to change at Robinhood’s push of a button. So although Robinhood promises the APY will go up if the fed cooperates, it could also be driven further down. Robinhood Cash Management is a great place to tuck away some money, maybe an emergency fund like the money I am putting away, that you don’t want to risk losing, but want to grow overtime. If you have cash sitting in your bank’s low interest yielding savings account, it is worth making the switch if you don’t actively use the money. I definitely will not make any transactions with the Robinhood debit card, as I always use credit cards to earn cash back. However, the savings account will prove very useful for money that I normally keep tucked away. Using your money to generate more, that's the name of the game, right?



Other Uses for Cash Management

Seamless Transfer from Checking to Investing - If Robinhood is your primary investing platform, I think Cash Management definitely has some use for you. If you are looking to invest directly with your paychecks, sending your direct deposits to Robinhood would make the process of trading/investing much easier by cutting out the process of transferring funds from your regular bank into Robinhood before being able to buy stock. I personally don’t use Robinhood as my primary brokerage, so setting up direct deposit wouldn’t get my money in the market any faster, and would serve no purpose.

Primary Bank Account – If you’re looking to transition into the world of mobile banking, Robinhood Cash Management is a great way to do so. Although I’m not a big proponent of online banking, it could be for you, so if you want to try it out, by all means, go ahead...you might love it!

Avoiding Fees – If you bank with a company that slams you with frequent fees, banking with Robinhood Cash Management will guarantee zero fees, unless you use an unregistered ATM. If I were forced to switch to a mobile banking platform, I would choose Robinhood. No monthly fees, no overdraft fees, and foreign transaction fees.
