Netflix and Its Role in The Streaming Wars

As the streaming wars begins to heat up, I give you my take on what could potentially be a bloodbath for today’s top streaming services, and its investors.

Posted by Dan Fooks on September 23, 2019

If you go back a few years, you will reach a period of time where Netflix was the big man on the block. At this time, there were no other serious competitors in the streaming industry, so you could say things were looking pretty good for Netflix with endlessly increasing subscriber counts, popular TV shows and movies, and the creation of original content back in early 2013. For years to come, Netflix dominated the streaming game. They even became a meme (“Netflix and Chill”), which actually, believe it or not, helped them gain publicity as a company among a younger demographic. As Netflix reached its peak value as a stock in July 2018 at a price of 418.97 per share, things were looking great for both the company and investors like myself (Netflix was my top performing stock in 2018). However, things are beginning to heat up amongst different competitors, who are giving Netflix a run for their money. Take a look at the following stats (via macrotrends.net):

- As of the market close on September 20, 2019, the latest closing price for NFLX is 270.75.

- The all-time high Netflix stock closing price was 418.97 on July 09, 2018.

- The Netflix 52-week high stock price is 386.80, which is 42.9% above the current share price.

- The Netflix 52-week low stock price is 231.23, which is 14.6% below the current share price.

- The 52-week average Netflix stock price is 332.19.

As you can see, Netflix isn’t exactly doing the best it could be since its price peaked in July 2018. Netflix now has some serious competition. The streaming wars has begun.

Netflix is now battling with multiple streaming services, including Hulu, Roku TV, Amazon Prime Video, YouTube TV, the newly announced Apple TV+ (which is at an affordable rate of $4.99 a month as opposed to Netflix’s monthly rate of $12.99), and even an upcoming streaming service from Disney, which will be extremely popular among millennials due to its rumored selection of classic Disney content. All of these streaming services offer both competitive rates and content, leaving many wondering how much longer Netflix will stay afloat. The fact that Netflix hasn’t been performing as well, as well as losing subscribers in the states has raised the eyebrows of many investors, and not for good reasons. At the end of the previous market close on Friday, NFLX fell over 5%, over 15 points. Roku, a new-ish competitor on the market offering Roku TV, falling over 19% on Friday. While many see this as a buying opportunity, others view this as the start of a large downtrend among streaming services due to increasing competition.

Where I Stand:

Despite recent doubts about Netflix as a competitor in the streaming game, I believe Netflix is currently undervalued as a company, and I believe that Netflix will soon see better days. Is it possible Netflix begins to crawl back towards its 52-week low of 231.23? Absolutely. However, I will be holding my position, as I believe the next two quarters will produce some upward movement. Last January, Netflix saw nothing but gains as the stock market as a whole bounced back from a small period of recession starting in October 2018. With Netflix approaching the fourth market quarter, could we see a similar rebound to kick off 2020? All in all, even if Netflix continues to decrease for a short period of time, I view the short-term future as a great buying opportunity to buy before a large uptrend. Netflix has a lot to save its future and distinguish itself from other services, including the production of many popular Netflix Original Series’, such as Stranger Things and The Society. But, at the same time, other services are also offering great content, so subscriber counts over the course of a year or two will tell who wins street cred for America’s favorite streaming service. My bet is that there is money to be made after a short period of decline for Netflix. Time will tell what is in store for the future of the company as it battles against new competitors on the block.
